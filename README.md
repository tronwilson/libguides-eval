# Overview

As LibGuides forms an integral part of a Library's communication with its patrons and stakeholders it is important that
we think carefully about how we create, present, manage and maintian our guides. In fact, an argument can be made that
LibGuides will take on a renewed releavance going forward in a world where the internet increasingly becomes saturated
with synthetic information produced by Large Language Models. In this case it might be more important than previoiusly
thought to maintain curated sources of information from non-synthetic sources in order to vouch for the authenticity and
originality of a work or idea.

## Scope

Therefore we want to re-evaluate our Libguides along three axes. (1) Who is our audience and what is the content that we
believe best serves their needs. (2) How do we present that content in a way that (2) aesheetically pleasing while (3)
giving them thorough covereage of the issue and balances the need to (a) access key information quickly and efficiently
and (b) have a thorough enough treatment of a given subject matter that the purposes of literacy and whatnot ar served.

### Audience & Purpose

By purpose obviously we mean what is the reason for the guide itself. Generally we are serving law facutly and law
students. Of course, as our guides are on the open web their is a wider audience that while not the pirmary one should
probably be taken into account. Essentially our guides can be broken down into a few categories.

1.  Subject Research - These are guides that help a law student or any other putative visitor educate zemself about a
    particular legal subject.
1.  Topical Guides - These guides might be Guides
1.  Class Specific Guides - These Guides should provide class specific information for a particular class being taught
    by a specific professor.

### Presentation of Content

How do we structure out content in such a way that it's both aesthetically pleasing and presents the information in a
way that is consistent with the expectations of the user based on the type of guide that is published? I don't think
that the aesthetics of the presentation should be overlooked or undervalued. One of the clear value-adds from a
Librarian is to put the work in to make it easier for the user to visually and electronically navigate the content and
move around the document in a way that's intuitive with less of the burden being placed on the user to surface relevant
information.

### Functionality & Accessibility
Functionality should be a key focus as well particularly when it comes to accessibility. This may create some tension in
that making a good looking website is not the same as making one that is functional and accessible. However, I believe
the goal should be to focus on presentation of the information while ensuring that functionality is not sacrificed to
that presentation. Or to put it another way we should always ensure that we're making the information available to the
broadest possible audience be the users sight impaired or that they may have difficulty navigating the site using a
mouse or inability to access the web content using the latest browser features, etc. But our initial goal should be to
make content that is easy and intuitive to navigate. 

## Conclusion
In conclusion it seems that there are three overriding factors that need to be taken into account when creating
LibGuides. We need to think about intended audience and purpose and this will guide how we structure the content and how
much we decide to put on the site. The answer to this question allows us to deal with the second issue of presentation.
Given our audience is likely to be X,Y, or Z what is the best way to struture the content so that they can easily and
intuitively navigate the content. But given any decisions we make in what kind of content we create and how we present
it we need always keep in mind accessibility or ensuring that we reduce the barriers to as close to zero as possible of
access to the information.
